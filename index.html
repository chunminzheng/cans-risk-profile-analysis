Key Findings

Two counterintuitive patterns surfaced through longitudinal trajectory and multi-incident analysis.

Finding 1
Group C's Suicide Risk Escalates — Despite Low Intake Scores
Group C enters care looking stable on suicide risk (mean 0.43 out of 3). But across five assessment cycles, that score quietly climbs to 0.59 — a 37% increase. Meanwhile Group A, the most alarming group at intake (1.09), steadily improves to 0.55. A low intake score does not mean low ongoing risk.

Finding 2
Group B Drives 44% of Complex Cases — Despite Moderate Scores
84 youth (7.9%) experienced incidents across 2+ distinct categories. Group B — with the 2nd-lowest risk behavior score at intake — contributes 44% of all complex cases and a higher within-group rate (11.7%) than Group A (10.5%). Functional impairment, not clinical severity, predicts case complexity.

Methodology note. Risk profiles were derived from K-means clustering (k=4, silhouette = 0.393) on three intake CANS domain means. Domain scores were standardized before clustering. Incident observation windows span intake to next assessment date, or 90 days if no follow-up existed. Trajectory analyses use all assessment records with profile labels fixed at intake. All analyses are associational — causal claims require further study.
