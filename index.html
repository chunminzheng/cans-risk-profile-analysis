Three findings that go beyond the profile labels

Knowing which group a youth belongs to is useful, but it is not the whole picture. These findings identify specific patterns — in how risks combine, how they change over time, and where incident burden is concentrated — that have direct implications for how cases are managed.

Finding 1 Risk Interaction

Depression and suicide risk together produce a 61% incident rate — not the 38% an additive model would predict

Youth who score high on both depression and suicide risk at intake have a 61% incident rate. If these two factors simply added up independently, we would expect around 38%. The actual rate is 23 percentage points higher — indicating a multiplicative interaction, not just two separate risk factors stacking.

Chi-square test across all four groups is statistically significant (p < .0001). The co-occurrence of depression ≥ 2 and suicide risk ≥ 2 is a stronger predictor of incidents than either score on its own.
Implication

Youth with both depression ≥ 2 and suicide risk ≥ 2 at intake should be flagged for joint review — treating these as two separate items underestimates the combined risk substantially.

Finding 2 Score Trajectory

Group C's suicide risk score rises across successive assessments while Group A's consistently declines

Group A enters care with the highest suicide risk score (1.09) and the highest incident rate (43%). Across five assessments, that score drops to 0.55 — suggesting services are having a measurable effect. Group C enters with a much lower score (0.43) and looks comparatively stable at intake. But their score rises to 0.59 by the fifth assessment — a 37% increase while in care. Intake scores alone do not capture this divergence.

Group C (light blue): Suicide risk rises from 0.43 at intake to 0.59 by the 5th assessment. Group A (dark blue) declines from 1.09 to 0.55 over the same period.
Implication

Group C youth warrant more frequent reassessment intervals. A low intake score is not sufficient justification for reduced monitoring — this group tends to escalate quietly over the course of service.

Finding 3 Incident Concentration

84 youth (8%) experienced multiple incident types — and 44% of them are from Group B, not the highest-acuity group

Most youth experienced zero or one type of serious incident. 84 youth experienced two or more distinct incident types — these are the most resource-intensive cases. Group B, which has moderate scores at intake and would not typically be prioritised for intensive intervention, accounts for 44% of this subgroup. Group C, which is the most clinically severe at intake, accounts for only 21%.

A youth is counted as multi-incident if they experienced 2 or more different incident types (e.g., a self-harm event and a police involvement in the same window). Group B contributed 37 of the 84 youth in this subgroup.
Implication

Youth with two or more incident types should be reviewed as a distinct high-burden subgroup, independent of their profile label. Group B's overrepresentation here suggests that functional impairment — not clinical severity — is the primary driver of case complexity.
